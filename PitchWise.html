The real problem

Most decks don't fail because they look bad. They fail because investors don't believe the story fast enough.

Investors pass when a deck is unclear, generic, unsupported, or poorly sequenced. The slide design is rarely the problem - the broken investor logic underneath it is. These are the failure points PitchWise helps you find and fix.

● The first slide is unclear ● The problem is too generic ● The wedge is vague ● Traction is under-labeled ● Market math feels inflated ● GTM isn't repeatable ● The competition slide is shallow ● The ask isn't tied to milestones ● The deck doesn't build belief slide by slide ● Weak slides get prettier, not clearer

01 — PitchWise Playbook

Find what is killing the deck

Most founders do not know why investors lose interest. They keep changing colors, layouts, and slide order while the real objection stays untouched.

The Playbook gives you the investor filter: 5-second clarity, 2-minute logic, and venture-scale. It shows you how to judge your deck the way a serious seed investor would.

You learn how to diagnose weak problem framing, vague wedges, unlabeled traction, inflated market logic, shallow competition slides, and unsupported asks.

The goal is simple: stop making a weak deck prettier and find the two or three slides actually costing you the meeting. Use it before running the AI skill files so you understand what the system is fixing and why.

03 — Pitch Template

Build on the right fundraising spine

A blank pitch template does not tell you what investors need to believe. It only gives you empty slides.

The PitchWise 18-slide template gives you the fundraising spine: definition, problem, alternatives, solution, product, why now, wedge, market, business model, traction, customers, GTM, competition, roadmap, revenue estimate, team, ask, and close.

Each slide has one job: reduce confusion, increase belief, or prove scale. That makes the deck easier to read, forward, and evaluate without you in the room.

The template is editable in PowerPoint, so the final deck remains practical, reusable, and founder-controlled. Use it after the copy is finalized so design supports the story instead of hiding gaps.

04 — Before/After Scorecard

Know if the new deck is actually better

Most founders finish a redesign and still do not know if the deck improved. A cleaner deck can still fail if the story is unclear.

The Before/After Scorecard lets you compare the original deck against the rebuilt version using investor-readiness criteria.

It checks clarity, problem urgency, narrative sequence, wedge focus, proof quality, market logic, GTM, differentiation, business model, founder-market fit, ask, and investor heat.

You see whether the new deck is actually stronger, or whether it only looks more polished. Use it before investor outreach so you are not sending a deck with unresolved objections and hoping no one notices.
